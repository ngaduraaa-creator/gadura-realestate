What Makes a Divorce Sale Different

  • Timing is dictated by the matrimonial case, not the market. Equitable distribution under New York Domestic Relations Law §236(B) controls when and how marital property is divided [1].
  • Both spouses must usually sign. If both names are on the deed, both signatures are required on the listing agreement and the eventual contract of sale, unless a court order directs otherwise.
  • Proceeds are escrowed. Net proceeds typically go into an escrow account held by an attorney or the court pending distribution.
  • Emotions affect negotiation. A neutral broker keeps price and terms on comps and market data, not leverage between spouses.

Queens & Long Island Divorce Sale FAQ

"Can one spouse list the home without the other?" Generally no, if both are on the deed. A court order can modify this. Your attorney will advise.

"What if we disagree on price?" Nitin prices based on documented OneKey® MLS comps. Both sides get the same data at the same time.

"What about showings when one spouse still lives there?" Showings scheduled to respect the occupying spouse's access and privacy.

"Who pays commission?" Commission comes out of seller proceeds at closing and is typically split in the settlement per the matrimonial agreement or court order. Commission is negotiable and set in writing in the listing agreement [2].

"What about capital gains?" The IRC §121 primary-residence exclusion may apply (up to $250,000 single / $500,000 married filing jointly) [3]. Speak with your CPA — this is tax, not real estate, advice.

NY Property Condition Disclosure (RPL §462): New York sellers of 1–4 family residential real property are required by the Property Condition Disclosure Act to deliver a completed Property Condition Disclosure Statement (PCDS) to the buyer before the buyer signs the purchase contract.
