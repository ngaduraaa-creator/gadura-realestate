Should You Use an iBuyer or Real Estate Agent in Queens?

By Nitin Gadura | Gadura Real Estate | Published June 2026

The promise of iBuyers is appealing: get an instant offer on your home, skip the showings, and close in two weeks. Companies like Opendoor and Offerpad have marketed this convenience aggressively in markets across the country. But does the iBuyer model actually work in Queens? And more importantly, does it work for your bottom line?

I am Nitin Gadura of Gadura Real Estate, and I have analyzed dozens of iBuyer transactions in the New York City market. The data tells a clear story: in almost every scenario, Queens homeowners net significantly more money by listing with a local agent than by selling to an iBuyer. But there are specific situations where speed and convenience genuinely outweigh the price difference.

This guide breaks down the real numbers so you can make an informed decision.

What Is an iBuyer?

An iBuyer (instant buyer) is a company that uses algorithms and automated valuation models to make instant or near-instant cash offers on homes. The seller gets a quick offer without listing the property, showing it, or negotiating with individual buyers. The iBuyer then resells the property at market value, profiting from the spread.

Major iBuyers and NYC Presence

  • Opendoor: The largest national iBuyer. Has expanded into parts of the NYC metro area but primarily focuses on single-family homes. Limited Queens coverage.
  • Offerpad: Smaller national iBuyer. Does not currently operate in NYC.
  • Local cash buyer companies: Several NYC-based investor groups and wholesalers operate iBuyer-style models in Queens, offering fast cash closings on properties in any condition.

It is important to distinguish between national iBuyer platforms (which use technology-driven pricing) and local cash buyers or cash home buyers in Queens (which are typically investor groups buying below market value for renovation and resale).

The Real Cost Comparison: iBuyer vs. Agent in Queens

Let us run the numbers on a $750,000 Queens home, which is close to the current median for a single-family property.

Selling with an iBuyer

  • iBuyer offer price: $690,000 to $712,500 (typically 5-8% below market value)
  • iBuyer service fee: $34,500 to $57,000 (5-8% of offer price)
  • Repair deductions: $10,000 to $25,000 (iBuyers deduct estimated repair costs from their offer)
  • Closing costs (seller portion): $8,000 to $12,000
  • Net proceeds to seller: approximately $596,500 to $660,500

Listing with a Local Queens Agent

  • Expected sale price: $740,000 to $770,000 (at or slightly above market value in a competitive market)
  • Agent commission: $37,000 to $46,200 (5-6% negotiable, split between buyer and seller agents)
  • Staging and prep costs: $2,000 to $5,000
  • Closing costs (seller portion): $8,000 to $12,000
  • Net proceeds to seller: approximately $677,000 to $707,000

The Bottom Line

Listing with an agent nets you $17,000 to $110,000 more than selling to an iBuyer on a $750,000 Queens home. The average difference across market conditions is approximately $50,000 to $70,000. That is real money, and it is the primary reason most financial advisors and real estate professionals recommend listing on the open market.

Timeline Comparison

iBuyer Timeline

  1. Day 1: Submit property details online or via phone
  2. Day 1-3: Receive preliminary offer
  3. Day 3-7: iBuyer inspection (may adjust offer downward)
  4. Day 7-14: Closing
  5. Total: 7 to 14 days

Traditional Agent Timeline

  1. Week 1: Pre-listing preparation (photos, staging, pricing strategy)
  2. Week 2-3: Active listing, showings, open houses
  3. Week 2-4: Offers received, negotiations completed
  4. Week 4-8: Contract to closing (buyer mortgage processing, inspections, appraisal)
  5. Total: 30 to 60 days

The speed difference is real: 7-14 days versus 30-60 days. For sellers who need to close urgently, this matters. For sellers who can wait 4-8 weeks, the additional time typically translates to $50,000 or more in net proceeds.

Queens-Specific Factors That Favor Agents Over iBuyers

The iBuyer model was built for homogeneous suburban markets where automated valuation models can accurately price cookie-cutter homes. Queens is the opposite of that.

Property Diversity

Queens has single-family detached homes, semi-attached row houses, two-family homes, three-family homes, co-ops, condos, and mixed-use properties, often on the same block. iBuyer algorithms struggle to price this diversity accurately, which is why their offers tend to be conservative (read: low) in Queens.

Neighborhood Micro-Markets

A home on the north side of Hillside Avenue in Jamaica can be worth significantly more than an identical home three blocks south. iBuyer algorithms do not capture these micro-market distinctions. A local agent who has sold 50 homes in your specific neighborhood does.

Multi-Family Premium

Two-family and three-family homes in Queens command a significant premium because of rental income potential. iBuyers typically undervalue this income stream. A local agent who can market the property's income-generating potential to investor buyers will consistently achieve a higher sale price. See my two-family house guide for more details.

Community Networks

Queens is community-driven. Buyers in Richmond Hill find homes through community networks, not algorithms. Buyers in Jackson Heights rely on trusted referrals. An agent embedded in these communities reaches buyers that no iBuyer platform can access.

When Selling to an iBuyer or Cash Buyer Makes Sense

Despite the financial disadvantage, there are legitimate situations where speed and simplicity outweigh maximizing price:

  • Job relocation with tight deadline: If your employer needs you in another city within 2-3 weeks, an iBuyer or cash buyer eliminates the risk of your home sitting on the market while you are gone.
  • Divorce requiring fast asset division: When both parties need to divide proceeds quickly, a fast sale can prevent prolonged legal disputes.
  • Severe financial distress: If you are facing foreclosure or cannot afford to maintain the property, a fast sale prevents further financial damage.
  • Estate/probate situations: Executors who need to liquidate an inherited property quickly may prioritize speed over maximum price.
  • Properties needing extensive repair: If a home needs $100,000+ in renovations, selling as-is to a cash buyer avoids the renovation investment and risk.

How to Evaluate Any Offer on Your Queens Home

Whether you receive an iBuyer offer, a cash buyer offer, or a traditional buyer offer, evaluate it on these factors:

  1. Net proceeds after all fees: This is the only number that matters. Compare what you actually receive, not the headline offer price.
  2. Contingencies: Inspection contingencies, appraisal contingencies, and financing contingencies all create risk. Fewer contingencies means more certainty.
  3. Timeline to close: How quickly will you have the money?
  4. Repair deductions: iBuyers and cash buyers frequently deduct repair costs after their initial inspection. Ask for the final, post-inspection offer before comparing.
  5. Certainty of close: What is the risk that the deal falls through? Cash offers with no contingencies have the highest certainty.
