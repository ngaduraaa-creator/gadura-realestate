Investment Property Guide: Queens & Brooklyn Real Estate

By Nitin Gadura | Gadura Real Estate | Updated April 2026

Why Invest in Queens and Brooklyn Real Estate?

Queens and Brooklyn represent two of the strongest real estate investment markets in the country. With consistent population growth, improving infrastructure, and strong rental demand from a diverse workforce, these boroughs offer exceptional opportunities for both new and experienced investors. Nitin Gadura of Gadura Real Estate specializes in helping investors identify high-ROI properties across both boroughs.

The key advantage of investing in Queens and Brooklyn is the combination of relatively affordable entry points compared to Manhattan with strong and growing rental demand. Multi-family homes, mixed-use buildings, and condos all present unique investment opportunities depending on your goals and budget.

Best Neighborhoods for Investment Properties

Queens Investment Hotspots

  • Jamaica: Major transit hub with ongoing development, strong rental demand, and multi-family housing stock. Prices remain accessible relative to western Queens neighborhoods.
  • Richmond Hill: Thriving Guyanese and South Asian community with strong demand for two-family and three-family homes that generate rental income.
  • Ozone Park: Affordable multi-family homes with proximity to JFK Airport. Popular with Indian and Guyanese families seeking multi-generational living arrangements.
  • Corona: Dense, transit-rich neighborhood with strong rental demand from working families. Affordable entry points for investors.
  • Ridgewood: Growing arts scene and spillover demand from Bushwick make this an appreciating market with multi-family housing stock.

Brooklyn Investment Hotspots

  • East New York: Significant city investment in rezoning and new development. Affordable entry points with strong appreciation potential.
  • Canarsie: Family-oriented neighborhood with affordable multi-family homes and a strong Caribbean community providing reliable rental demand.
  • Bushwick: Continued gentrification and arts-driven demand make multi-family properties here increasingly valuable.
  • Flatbush: Large Caribbean community, affordable compared to northern Brooklyn, and excellent transit access driving appreciation.
  • Brownsville: Among the most affordable areas in Brooklyn with city-backed revitalization initiatives underway.

Types of Investment Properties

Multi-Family Homes (2-4 Units)

The most popular investment vehicle in Queens and Brooklyn. Two-family and three-family homes allow you to live in one unit and rent the others, or rent all units for maximum cash flow. FHA financing is available for owner-occupied properties up to four units, allowing down payments as low as 3.5%.

Mixed-Use Properties

Buildings with commercial space on the ground floor and residential above. Common along busy corridors like Jamaica Avenue, Liberty Avenue in Richmond Hill, and Flatbush Avenue in Brooklyn. These provide diversified income streams.

Condos and Co-ops

Individual units purchased for rental income. Co-ops in Queens often have restrictions on subletting, so check board policies. Condos generally offer more flexibility for investors. Contact Nitin Gadura at (917) 705-0132 to discuss which property type fits your investment strategy.

Key Metrics for NYC Real Estate Investors

  • Cap Rate: Net operating income divided by purchase price. In Queens and Brooklyn, cap rates typically range from 4% to 7% depending on neighborhood and property condition.
  • Cash-on-Cash Return: Annual pre-tax cash flow divided by total cash invested. Aim for 6%+ in outer borough markets.
  • Gross Rent Multiplier: Purchase price divided by annual gross rent. Lower GRM indicates better value. Queens and Brooklyn often offer GRMs of 12-18.
  • Vacancy Rate: NYC has historically low vacancy rates (under 5%), making rental income more predictable than many other markets.

Financing Your Investment Property

NYC investment properties can be financed through conventional mortgages (typically requiring 20-25% down for non-owner-occupied), FHA loans (3.5% down for owner-occupied 1-4 units), portfolio loans from local banks, and hard money loans for fix-and-flip projects.
