## VIDEO 7: Co-op vs Condo in NYC — Which Is Better?

**Thumbnail Text:** CO-OP vs CONDO: The Truth  
**Length:** ~4 minutes spoken

### Script

[VISUAL: Nitin standing outside a mid-rise residential building in Queens]

Co-op or condo — if you're buying in NYC, you need to understand the difference before you make a move. I'm Nitin Gadura with Gadura Real Estate and I'm going to break this down simply so you know exactly which one is right for you.

[VISUAL: Text on screen — "What's a Co-op?"]

Let's start with co-ops. When you buy a co-op, you're not buying real property. You're buying shares in a corporation that owns the building. Those shares come with a proprietary lease that gives you the right to live in your specific unit. About 75 percent of the residential housing stock in New York City is co-ops. They tend to be more affordable upfront than condos.

[VISUAL: Text on screen — "What's a Condo?"]

A condo is real property. When you buy a condo, you own your unit outright. You get a deed. You pay real estate taxes directly. You have more freedom with what you can do with your unit — renovations, subletting, selling.

[VISUAL: Nitin seated, side-by-side comparison graphic on screen]

Here's where the differences really matter. Approval process — co-ops have a board. You have to submit a purchase application with financial documents, references, and sometimes do an in-person board interview. The board can reject you, and in most cases they don't have to give a reason. Condos typically have a right of first refusal, but the approval process is much simpler and faster.

[VISUAL: Text comparison continuing]

Subletting — most co-ops have strict subletting rules. Some don't allow it at all. Some allow it after a certain number of years with board approval. Condos generally allow subletting with fewer restrictions. If you're an investor or if there's any chance you might want to rent your unit out, this matters a lot.

Financing — co-ops often require a larger down payment, sometimes 20 percent or more. Some co-ops don't allow financing at all and require an all-cash purchase. Condos are generally more flexible with financing options including FHA loans.

Monthly costs — co-ops have maintenance fees that cover your share of the building's expenses, including property taxes. Condos have common charges plus your own separate property tax bill. When you compare monthly costs, make sure you're comparing apples to apples.

[VISUAL: Nitin to camera]

So which one is right for you? If you want lower upfront cost and you're okay with board rules and restrictions, a co-op can be a great deal. If you want flexibility — especially if you might want to rent it out or you need FHA financing — a condo is usually the better choice. And if you want even more space and independence, we should look at one- and two-family houses, which Queens has plenty of.
